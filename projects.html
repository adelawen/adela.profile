Adaptive Signal Compensation
for Vulnerable Pedestrian Systems

NSTC SubmittedPTV Vissim Python COMAutoCADSignal Control

The engineering problem nobody was solving: VIPS systems protect elderly pedestrians with a 10-second green extension — but each random trigger pulse-shocks a precisely timed arterial system. Upstream vehicles hit red lights; downstream intersections open to empty roads. Research shows 40–50% bandwidth loss per event, yet SCOOT/SCATS are too slow to recover within 1–3 cycles. No prior work offered a local, physics-aware compensation strategy for this exact disruption pattern.
The Challenge — & What I Did About It

Over months of discussions with my advisor, I narrowed a vague interest in pedestrian safety down to a precise, literature-supported gap — then had to build the entire technical stack to test it.

PTV Vissim is a paid, professional microsimulation platform. No course teaches it. So I went online, read documentation, watched tutorials, and built the environment from scratch. When I realized the road network needed to match the exact 400m intersection spacing in the proposal, I taught myself AutoCAD to draft geometrically accurate base maps — because if the geometry was wrong, every downstream result would be wrong too.

Writing the proposal taught me: a strong research document doesn't lead with methodology. It leads with pain. Why does this gap exist? What does inaction cost? Only then does the solution earn its place.

Proposed Framework

Three mechanisms working together:

  • Spatial Compensation — Robertson dispersion model dynamically corrects downstream offsets to intercept the dispersed platoon front, rather than assuming it arrives as a block.
  • Multi-cycle Linear Recovery — spreads the 10-second phase error across N=3 cycles, eliminating abrupt jerk and oscillation that rigid synchronization causes.
  • System Defense Layer — minor-street slack absorption + dynamic node decoupling prevent local disruption from cascading corridor-wide.
Key Outcomes
  • Submitted 10-page NSTC proposal — January 2026 — after multiple full rewrites with faculty advisor.
  • 6-scenario comparison framework: V/C = 0.4 / 0.7 / 0.9, 10 replications each.
  • Python automation: 50+ scenario processing 3 days → 2 hours.
  • Completed Taiwan Academic Ethics certification for grant eligibility.

The gap between narrative and numbers: Logistics decarbonization fills policy documents — but most analysis stays qualitative. The quantitative link between "we'll adopt more green vehicles" and "here is how many tonnes of CO₂ that actually prevents" is missing. This research builds that link for DHL Taiwan's last-mile operations.
Selection & What Got Me There

Dr. Chen Tao's MOE Study Abroad program is assessed holistically. Two things stood out:

The research itself — not a course project. I chose a topic at the intersection of international logistics policy, governance frameworks, and engineering carbon accounting to understand how sustainability commitments actually translate into measurable outcomes.

IELTS 6.5 — demonstrating readiness to operate independently at a Russell Group university. Writing this research also sharpened my ability to distill complex cross-disciplinary findings into a compelling narrative — a skill that fed directly into writing the NSTC proposal.

Research Scope
  • International Policy Context — mapped global regulatory pressures and DHL's GoGreen roadmap against Taiwan's last-mile logistics market structure.
  • Public-Private Governance — analyzed how government incentives and corporate commitments interact — and where they misalign — in driving fleet electrification.
  • User Demand Modeling — modeled adoption likelihood and willingness-to-pay for green delivery options using behavioral economics frameworks.
  • Quantitative Carbon Projection — built mathematical models predicting emission reductions under varying green transport penetration rates, converting policy narratives into engineering-grade estimates.
Connection to NSTC Work

Both projects ask the same question: what does it take to make a system change that actually holds? One through signal engineering. The other through carbon accounting and governance. Same instinct — different scale.
